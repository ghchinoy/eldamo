Morphology of Quenya Perfect

by Paul Strack (2025)
Note: the information in this paper was originally presented at Omentielva Nertea in 2022. I wrote this article in 2025 using my 2022 notes. I took the opportunity to correct a few errors in the original presentation, but I have not updated the examples in the main article to include anything published after 2022. However, I feel that my original conclusions (barring the corrected errors) are largely consistent with the material published up through 2025.

It is well known that the Quenya language invented by Tolkien included various verb tenses, and one of the better known tenses is the Quenya perfect, as seen in phrases like utúvienyes from The Lord of the Rings. There have been several previous studies of the Quenya perfect, including:

  • The Quenya Perfect Tense, Thorsten Renk 2007, http://www.science-and-fiction.org/elvish/quenya_perfect_tense.html
  • Quenya Compound Tenses, Helios De Rosario Martínez 2018, https://www.elvish.org/Tengwestie/articles/DeRosarioMartinez/quenyacompoundtenses.phtml

Although Martínez’s paper did not discussion Tolkien’s later conception of the perfect, Martínez did described the 1920s perfect tense which was a compound tense. Renk’s paper was fairly comprehensive, but a great amount of new material on verbs appeared in Parma Eldalamberon #22, published in 2015. It is worth revisiting the Quenya perfect to incorporate information from that publication and other documents published after 2007.

Overview of the Quenya Perfect

Linguistically, the perfect is generally viewed an aspect of a verb rather than a verb tense itself, since it is not inherently associated with time. In many languages, the perfect describes a completed action. In Tolkien’s words:

Perfect. This described an action or process that was completed in the immediate past, but the effects of which are still present (PE22/130).

This can be contrasted with the simple past, which describes actions that occurred at some undefined point in the past. Compare:

  • She lived in the city for 10 years (at some point in the past).
  • She has lived in the city for 10 years (up until to now).

In Quenya, the ordinary perfect was a verb tense with its own inflection. The basic form and function of the Quenya perfect has been known for a long time, since there are several example of it in The Lord of the Rings itself. One of the best-known examples is Aragorn’s exclamation Utúvienyes “I have found it!” After removing pronominal suffixes, this has the form utúvie “have found”. The basic verb stem for this example is known to be tuv- “to find” (PE17/110). Thus from this example we can already see the three basic features of the Quenya perfect inflection:

  • A verbal augment: utúvie.
  • A lengthened stem vowel: utúvie.
  • An -ie suffix: utúvie.

The verbal augment is optional and may be omitted, especially in poetry. Two representative examples appear in the Namárie poem, which had vánie “has passed” in the 1st edition of The Lord of the Rings, revised to avánie in the 2nd edition. As described by Tolkien in the Quendi and Eldar essay compared around 1959-60:

The form vánie appearing in verse has no augment: probably a phonetic development after a preceding vowel; but such forms are not uncommon in verse (WJ/366).

Vowel lengthening was likewise not always possible, particularly before consonant clusters. The perfect form of verbs like nahta- generally had only the augment and the -ie suffix: perfect anahtie (PE17/77, PE22/159).

Early Iterations of the Quenya Perfect

There are a few examples of possible perfects in The Lost Tales of the 1910s:

  • tulielto “they have come” (LT1/114).
  • i·Eldar tulier “the Eldar have come” (LT1/114).
  • i·kal’antúlien “light hath returned” (LT1/184).

Not much is known of the verb system of the 1910s, since we have no Qenya grammar from this period. One challenge in interpreting early perfects is that the suffix -ie was a feature of the Quenya past tense in the 1910s and 1920s. While there were definitely past forms in the 1910s without the suffix -ie, we cannot be absolutely certain that these early “perfects” were not past forms. Past tenses with lengthened vowels were also common in the 1910s, for example the preterate (past form) tūre of the verb turu- “can, is able” from the Qenya Lexicon (PE12/95).

There was a chart of Qenya Verb Forms verb forms tucked into the Qenya Lexicon (PE14/28-30), but the only conjugations are for the present, past, future, and aorist tenses; there is no description of the perfect or imperfect. In this document, the simple past ended in e, as in tūle, but with pronominal additions the suffix became -ie, as in masculine past tūlien(do) and plural past tūlier. This further calls into question the “perfects” from The Lost Tales, which likewise had pronominal or plural suffixes.

By the Early Qenya Grammar (EQG) of the 1920s, the suffix -ie was a feature of all past forms, such simple past túlie “*came” (PE14/57). In this document, the perfect was a compound tense, either e tulien “has come” (lit. “is having come”) or suffixed tuliende; in this document e- was the Qenya verb for “to be” (PE14/57).

We do see some more “perfect-like” forms in the 1930s, such as lantier “they fell” and ullier “poured” in the Lament of Atalante (LR/47), and kárier or kárielto “they made” vs. káre “made” in Firiel’s Song (LR/72). However, these could easily be inflected vs. uninflected past forms as seen in the 1910s. So far as I know, there are no suffixed past forms from the 1930s without ie. Furthermore, in the first version of the Tengwesta Qenderinwa from around this period, Tolkien did say that the suffix -yē could be used in the past tense:

On the other hand n-infixion and n-suffixion remained concurrently in use, and often performed identical functions: as in the strong past tenses formed with either n-infix + suffix e, yē, or with suffix ne, nyē, as √KAT pa.t. *ka-n-tē beside √KAR pa.t. *kar-nē (PE18/46).

The first time we see truly unambiguous perfect forms is in Lord of the Rings drafts of the 1940s. Examples like ettulielle and tūlier in the draft Namárie poem are still ambiguous and might be past forms (TI/285). However, Aragorn’s túvien >> utúvienyes “I have found it” is clearly a perfect (SD/57). This second example coincides with a comprehensive description of the perfect appearing in the Quenya Verbal System (QVS) from 1948 (PE22/99-127).

Late-Style Perfects

Perfects from 1948 forward seem to be part of the same conceptual stratum. Descriptions and formations thereafter mostly use the three element pattern mentioned previously: augment, lengthened base vowel, and the -ie suffix. The first clear description of the last-style perfect is in Quendian & Common Eldarin Verbal Structure from the late 1940s (EVS1: PE22/92-98). A similar description appears Common Eldarin: Verb Structure from the early 1950s (EVS2: PE22/128-140):

The formations usually regarded as “perfect” were marked by lengthening of the base-vowel (never by fortification), and addition to the stem of a suffix: jē, ijē. There was also a different but allied form with augmented base, with short base vowel and suffix -jē. Thus from √MAT: mātiē or amatje; from √TUL: tūlie or utulje. There were also possibly already in Common Eldarin mixed forms, such as amātiē, utūliē (but not matjē etc.).

The augment was probably more frequently used in words where the “perfect” form naturally referred rather to the past than to the present: so amatjē “has eaten”; but tūlijē “has come, is here” ...

In Quenya ... the perfect was normally augmented, and as a rule showed lengthened stem with suffix -iē [my emphasis] (PE22/130-131).

The Quenya Verbal System (QVS) of 1948 probably has more information about the perfect than any other published document. In that document Tolkien said “for the [primitive] Perfect the formation amā́tiyḕ was standardized” (PE22/101).

The Perfect Augment

QVS said that “the augment was the sundóma [base vowel] of ‘strong’ verbs” (PE22/112). QVS also described several variations on how the perfect augment might manifest. Similar variations were described in other documents like EVS2.

One case is Quenya verbs whose primitive roots had a nasal-stop cluster at the beginning of the ancient verb stem, which became simple nasals in the modern form of the word. For example: √NDAK “slay” > Q. nak- “to slay” (nd- > n-). Such clusters could be restored in older perfects, such as in andákie. However, in “modern” (Tarquesta) Quenya, the perfects were usually reformed to match the modern verb stem: anákie. The same was true of Quenya verbs whose the initial consonant might have undergone differing phonological development when appearing between vowels: √SAL had Q. perfect asálie rather than azálie > **arálie (PE12/133).

A second case are verb stems that begin with a vowel, where the augment is “absorbed” by the initial vowel: √UL “pour”: u-ūlijē > úlie “has poured” (PE22/133). However, sometimes the entire verb stem was reduplicated: ulúlie “has poured” (PE22/112). In QVS, Tolkien said such reduplicated forms only occurred in verse, but in EVS2 from the early 1950s he said they could occur in ordinary speech as well (PE22/133).

A final case are verb stems with a prefix. Since the augment was optional, prefixed verbs might omit the augment, but the only undeleted example of such an augmentless prefixed verb published prior to 2022 is the long perfect form ehtelunelye (see later in this article for a discussion of the long perfect). Most of the examples published prior to 2022 have the augment inserted between the prefix and the verb stem: ehtelu- “bubble up” (et + kelu) → perfect etekélie and orhal- “to exalt” (or + hal-) → perfect {orhálie >>} orahallie (PE22/103)[1].

Verb Classes from QVS

To further explore Quenya perfects, we first need a better understanding of Quenya verb classes. QVS has the best descriptions of verb classes in documents published as of 2025. In QVS, Tolkien divided verbs into two broad groups: “strong” verbs derived directly from a bi-consonantal verbal root and “weak” verbs that added some suffix or other variation to the root. Personally I prefer the terms “basic” and “derived” for these two groups, to avoid confusion with strong and weak pasts which could appear in either group.

Tolkien divided derived verbs into further subclasses (PE22/113-114):

  • Half-strong verbs: Notable for having half-strong pasts (see below), half-strong verbs had two distinct origins:
    • TALAT-stem verbs: From tri-consonantal roots: √TALAT > talta- “fall down”.
    • Formative verbs: Usually-intransitive verbs produced with the suffix -yă or -tă: √KAY + tă > caita- “lie”.
  • A-verbs: Verbs that were inherently continuous, with suffixal -ā: √PHAR + ā > fara- “hunt”.
  • U-verbs: Often-inceptive verbs with suffixal -ū: et + √KEL + u > ehtelu- “bubble up”.
  • Causatives: Transitive verbs with the sense “make or cause to X”, formed with suffixal -tā or (more rarely) -yā: √OR + tā > orta- “raise” = “cause to rise”.
  • Derivative verbs: Verbs based on nouns or adjectives with or without a suffix: NINKWE + nta > ninqinta- “to whiten, become white”.

Within these verb classes, we see several specialized perfect forms that might be perceived as “irregular”. The terms and classifications used in this article for these specialized perfects are my own rather than from Tolkien:

  • Strong Perfect: Formed directly from verb’s root with all suffixes removed.
  • Weak Perfects: Formed from weak past tenses.
  • Reformed Perfects: Formed from strong or half-strong past tenses.

These perfect formations were often in competition with each other within verb classes or even for individual verbs. These specialized perfect forms will be explored in the remainder of this article.

Past vs. Perfect

To understand weak and reformed perfects, we much first briefly explain weak, strong, and half-strong pasts.

The normal past tense for Quenya basic verbs was produced by nasal infixion or suffixion: mat- “eat” → mante “ate”; car- “do” → carne “did” (PE22/101-103). Tolkien called this the “strong” past, especially for nasal-infixed forms.

The “weak” past was produced by suffixal -nē, and was common in causative and derivative verbs: orta- “raise” → ortane “raise” (PE22/117).

The “half-strong” past was common in half-strong verbs, and was produced by nasal infixion before the third consonant of a tri-consonantal verbal root, or before the consonant of a ya- or ta-formative suffix.

  • talta- “fall down” (√TALAT) → past tala-n-t-ē > talante “fell down” (PE22/115).
  • sirya- “flow” (√SIR + yă) → past siri-n-y-ē > sirinye “flowed” (PE22/115).

The “strong” past could also appear in derived verbs by first removing any suffixes and using the strong past of the verbal root: koita- “live” → koine “lived” (PE22/103). Such strong pasts were especially common with formative verbs produced from roots ending in W or Y.

Quenya past forms are quite irregular, and the various past formations do not always align with verb classes. Some verbs with specialized pasts were eventually reformed to weak pasts, for example weak past taltane replacing half-strong talante (PE17/186). Some derived verbs had either half-strong or strong pasts produced directly from their root, for example formative verb caita- had either half-strong past cëante (< kay-a-n-t-e from the regular sound change aya > ëa) or strong past caine (PE22/157).

Perfect forms see similar variations. This is because there was a tendency in Quenya for past and perfect forms to align with each other. As Tolkien said in his Quendi and Eldar essay from 1959-60: “the forms of past and perfect became progressively more closely associated in Quenya” (WJ/366).

Sometimes this meant that obscure past forms could be replaced by forms based on the perfect. This was especially common with basic verbs whose stems ended in B. In the course of Quenya phonetic history, this b became v in most verb tenses, but tended to be preserved in nasal-infixed pasts:

  • √LAB “lick” > Q. lav- with perfect alávie (PE22/102).
  • (Archaic) nasal-infixed past lambe (PE22/102) or nasal-suffixed past lamne (PE22/151).

Since the relationship between the verb stem and its past were obscured by these sound changes, there was a tendency to use past forms with vowel-lengthening modeled after the perfect: láve “licked”, as seen in the Namárië poem from The Lord of the Rings.

A similar phenomenon could happen in reverse with perfects aligning to past forms, as discussed in the following sections.

Simple Perfects

The most common perfect pattern for derived verbs is the one described earlier in this article, which I label the “simple perfect”. Consider, for example:

  • fara- “hunt”, perfect afárie (PE22/164).

It involves the same three elements seen in the perfects of basic verbs:

  • A verbal augment: afárie.
  • A lengthened stem vowel: afárie.
  • An -ie suffix: afárie.

The only difference is that the -ie suffix replaces the final vowel of the verb stem of derived verbs. In the case of derived verbs, the stem vowel of derived verbs is often followed by a consonant cluster which inhibits vowel lengthening:

  • tulta- “send”, perfect utultie (PE22/156).

In derived verbs, the perfect suffix -ie is the result of normal phonetic development resulting from the combination of the vowel a with the primitive perfect suffix -(i)yē. There is a good example of this development for verb ortā- “raise” in notes from 1969 (PE22/159).

  • The most ancient perfect form was *orortā̆iyē with the usual perfect suffix -iye.
  • This first simplified to oro[r]taye.
  • The aye ending then underwent its usual phonological development > ëe > ie.

In the case of half-strong verbs, the final vowel was probably replaced primitively. There are several attested examples of this for the perfects of the TALAT-stem verb talta-:

  • *atā̆lati[y]e > ataltie (PE17/186).
  • *atlātiyē (PE18/88).
  • *atalāti[y]e > ataltie (PE22/134).

Examples of simple perfects can be found in all verb classes:

  • Basic: tul- “come”, perfect utúlie (S/190).
  • Formative: caita- “lie”, perfect acaitie (PE22/159).
  • TALAT-stem: talta- “fall down”, perfect ataltie (PE17/186).
  • A-verb: ala- “grow”, perfect (al)álie (PE22/164).
  • Causative: orta- “raise”, perfect orortie (PE22/157).
  • Derivative: tengwa- “read”, perfect etengwie (VT49/48)[2].
  • U-verb: ehtelu- “bubble up”, perfect etekélie (PE22/164).

This last verb happens to be the only published example of a u-verb with an attested perfect form. Its phonological developments are unclear. It is dangerous to extrapolate from a single example, but right now this is the only unambiguous u-verb perfect that we have.

There seems to be some specialized behavior for the simple perfects of verb stems ending in ya. In particular, the y is “absorbed” by the perfect suffix -ie in all attested examples:

  • melya- (√MEL “love”), perfect emēlie (PE17/77).
  • orya- “rise” (√OR), perfect orōrie (PE22/133).
  • sirya- “flow” (√SIR), perfect isīrie (PE22/164).
  • ulya- “pour” (√UL), perfect (ul)úlie (PE22/112).

These are conceivably examples of “strong perfects”, as discussed in the next section. With ya-verbs, however, the loss of y is universal. More likely this is the result of normal sound changes, since y cannot be followed by i in Quenya.

Strong Perfects

Sometimes the perfects of derived verbs are based directly on the verb’s root without any suffix. In this case, the perfect is formed by discarding the verb suffix and constructing an ordinary perfect from the root as if it were a basic verb. Such verbs often have strong pasts based directly on the root as well. One example of this is the verb fanta- “veil” = PHAN + tă . It has a perfect afānie, an archaic strong past †fāne and a modern weak past fantane. As Tolkien described it:

√PHAN ... fanta-, to “veil, cloak, mantle”. One of the verbs that though formed from primitive “verbal” bases had a present stem formed with the suffix -tă. The past was usually “strong” sc. without this suffix. So past fāne-, perfect afānie-, but later a past derived from the causatives (with suffix -tā) was often substituted: fantane- (PE17/179-180).

Most of the unambiguous examples of strong perfects come from ta-formatives, though as noted in the previous section the strong perfects of ya-formatives are indistinguishable from their simple perfects due to the loss of y. Examples include:

  • anta- “give” → strong perfect ānie, archaic strong past †āne and modern weak past antane (PE17/147)[3].
  • auta- “depart” → strong perfect oávie < awāwie (PE22/164).
  • caita- “lie” → strong perfect †acáye, strong past caine (PE22/159).
  • fanta- “veil” → strong perfect afāniē, archaic strong past †fāne and modern weak past fantane (PE22/159).
  • ista- “know” → strong perfect isísie, archaic strong past †inse and modern half-strong past isinte (PE22/159).
  • menta- “proceed” → strong perfect emēnie, strong past mennē (PE17/93).
  • nahta- “slay” → strong perfect anákie, strong past nanke or half-strong past nakante (PE22/157).
  • orta- “rise” → strong perfect orórie (PE22/159) vs. simple perfect orortie (PE22/164).

As noted previously, almost all of these examples have a strong past, though in many cases the strong past is archaic (†). One exception is auta- “depart” with archaic half-strong past †oante vs. an alternate strong past váne based on an inverted variant √WA of its is root √AW (WJ/366). The other example orta- “rise” mostly had only a half-strong past oronte, though in one place it had a half-strong past formed from its inverted root: †ronte (PE22/115).

Weak Perfects

Weak perfects apply only to verbs with weak pasts, which are mostly causative and derivative verbs. A weak perfect is formed directly from the weak past by adding an augment to the past form and changing the final vowel to -ie. In the case of causative verbs, there are examples of both simple and weak perfects:

  • orta- “raise” → weak perfect (or)ortanie (PE17/77, PE22/157) vs. simple perfect orortie (PE22/157, 159, 164).
  • tulta- “send” → weak perfect utultanie (PE22/157) vs. simple perfect utultie (PE22/164).

Most of the attested perfects of derivative verbs (formed from nouns and adjectives) are also weak perfects:

  • henta- “examine” → weak perfect ehentănie (PE17/77).
    • From noun hen(d)- “eye”, hence literally “to eye”.
  • karanya- “redden” → weak perfect (a)karanyanie (PE22/117).
  • ninqita- “whiten” → weak perfect (i)niñqitanie (PE22/117).

The one exception is tengwa- “read” with simple perfect etengwie[4] (VT49/48). It may be significant that this derivative verb is based directly on the noun tengwa “letter” without any suffix. The verb ninquita- “whiten” also has a simple perfect ininquitie in notes from 1969, but in that document it is an intransitive ta-formative with a half-strong past ninquinte, meaning “become white” rather than “make white” (PE22/157).

Reformed Perfects

There are examples of perfect forms based directly on strong or half-strong pasts, no doubt because “the forms of past and perfect became progressively more closely associated in Quenya” (WJ/366). I call these “reformed perfects”. This formation is most prevalent in half-strong verbs:

  • auta- “depart” → reformed perfect oantie, half-strong past oante (WJ/366).
  • auta- “depart” → reformed perfect avānie, strong past vāne (WJ/366).
  • ista- “know” → reformed perfect isintie, strong past sinte (PE17/77) vs. simple perfect (is)istie (PE22/164).
  • kaita- “lie” → reformed perfect akainie, strong past kaine vs. simple perfect akaitie (PE22/157).
  • orta- “rise” → reformed perfect orontie, half-strong past oronte (PE22/115) vs. simple perfect orortie (PE22/164).
  • talta- “fall” → reformed perfect atalantie, half-strong past talante vs. simple perfect ataltie (PE17/186).

This last example might have motivated this particular formation, since Tolkien used Atalantë as a name of Númenor, also serving as a pun on the name of Atlantis.

In QVS written in 1948, there are a number of examples of reformed perfects for basic verbs as well:

  • ham- “sit” → ahámie vs. reformed ahammie, past hamme (PE22/103).
  • kap- “jump” → archaic †akápie vs. reformed akampie, past kampe (PE22/102).
  • ken- “see” → ekénie vs. reformed ekennie, past kenne (PE22/103).
  • lah- “kick” → [deleted] alahie vs. reformed alahtie, past lahte (PE22/103).
  • mat- “eat” → archaic †amátie vs. reformed amantie, past mante (PE22/102).
  • ruk- “pluck” → urúkie vs. reformed uruñkie, past ruñke (PE22/102).
  • ton- “tap” → otónie vs. reformed otonnie, past tonne (PE22/103).

I can find no examples of such reformed perfects after 1948 in the currently published works of Tolkien (as of 2025).

Long Perfects

A final peculiarity of the verb system presented in QVS was a variant perfect form that Tolkien called the “long perfect” (PE22/104). The long perfect was formed by adding the suffix -lye to the past tense, and was mostly used in verse. Examples include:

  • ehtelu- “bubble up” → long perfect ehtelunelye (PE22/103).
  • kar- “make” → long perfect akarnelye (PE22/109).
  • ninqita- “whiten” → long perfect niñkwitanelye (PE22/117).
  • ninqinta- “become white” → long perfect niñqintantelye (PE22/117).
  • orta- “raise” → long perfect ortanelye (PE22/117).

This long perfect formation resembles the past active participle in QVS from which it seems to be derived: karnelya “*having made” (PE22/109-110). It also resembles the pluperfect formation in QVS: karnelyane “I was having made” = “I had made” (PE22/109).

I can find no signs of this long perfect after 1948.

Conclusion

Tolkien established the fundamental rules for Quenya perfect formation by 1948, and seems to have stuck with them thereafter. There are numerous “simple perfects” that adhere quite closely to this pattern when inflecting the verb stem: verbal augment, stem-vowel lengthening (if possible), and suffix -ie (replacing the final vowel of the stem of derived verbs).

There are, however, a number of “irregular” perfects following alternate patterns. In the case of reformed and weak perfects, these seem to be the result of Quenya pasts and perfects becoming “more closely associated”, since those perfects are based on the past form rather than the verb stem. The strong perfects might also derive from past/perfect alignment since like strong pasts they are based directly on the verb’s root, but there are some complications. In particular, there are examples of strong perfects without a corresponding strong past, and even if there is a strong past the corresponding strong perfect does not perfectly align with it:

  • orta- “rise” → strong perfect orórie but no strong past **orne (PE22/159).
  • menta- “proceed” → strong perfect emēnie not **emennie (PE17/93).
  • caita- “lie” → strong perfect †acáye vs actual reformed perfect acainie (PE22/159).

Outside of basic verbs, the choice of perfect formation often seems to be the result of historical accident, which makes the Quenya perfect formation quite irregular, much like its past forms.
