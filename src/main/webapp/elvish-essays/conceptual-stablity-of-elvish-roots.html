Conceptual Stability of Elvish Roots

by Paul Strack (2025)
Note: the information in this paper was originally presented at Omentielva Nertea in 2022. I wrote this article in 2025 using my 2022 notes. I did not refresh it with new Elvish roots published after that date, but I think the basic conclusions of this paper still hold.

The seed for this article came out of my analysis of Elvish roots for the Eldamo database in 2021. In that analysis, I trace the conceptual development of each root in Tolkien’s works published prior to that point, arranging them in chains from earlier to later forms. This arrangement is necessarily subjective. However, it can be used to examine the degree of conceptual continuity from Tolkien’s earlier to later ideas on his languages.

It is well known that Tolkien worked on his Elvish languages throughout most of his life, and his conception of these languages evolved over that time. Some of the elements of the languages remained the same, such as:

  • The definite article i “the” [1].
  • The split into “Welsh-like” languages (Gnomish, Noldorin, Sindarin) and “Finnish-like” languages (Quenya).
  • Individual words like Q. alcar, S. aglar “glory”.

Other elements of his languages underwent radical changes, such as:

  • The evolution of the “Welsh-like” languages from Gnomish through Noldorin and ultimately to Sindarin.
  • Numerous other features of grammar and vocabulary.

To get a feeling for the degree of change, we can compare poems associated with Tolkien’s talk on his “Secret Vice” (MC/198-223) composed around 1930 with later versions of the same poems composed in the 1950s and 60s.

For example, the final version of the Oilima Markiya poem circa 1930 (MC/213-214) began as follows:

Man kiluva kirya ninqe
oilima ailinello lúte,
níve qímari ringa ambar
ve maiwin qaine?

Who shall see a white ship
leave the last shore,
the pale phantoms in her cold bosom
like gulls wailing?

His revised Markirya poem from the 1960s (MC/221-222) had many differences:

Men* kenuva fáne kirya
métima hrestallo kíra,
i fairi néke ringa súmaryasse
ve maiwi yaimie?

* The word Men appeared as Man in later stanzas in the 1960s version.

The Nieninqe poem likewise had a version finished circa 1930 (MC/215-216), and began as follows:

Norolinde pirukendea
elle tande Nielikkilis,
tanya wende nieninqea
yar i vilya anta miqilis.

Tripping lightly, whirling lightly
thither came little Niéle,
that maiden like a snowdrop
to whom the air gives kisses?

And another version written in 1955 that was quite similar (PE16/96):

Norolinda pirukendea
lende tanna Nielikkilis,
sana wende nieninquea
yan i wilyar antar miquelis.

As another example of stability, we can see that Tolkien established the names of Valar very early in his writings, and they remained largely the same throughout his life. For example, the male Valar from the The Lost Tales (LT1) were Manwe, Ulmo, Aule, Orome, Tulkas, Mandos, and Lórien. Tolkien was still using the same names in the materials used for the published version of The Silmarillion (S). Likewise, the female Valie names in The Lost Tales were Varda, Yavanna, Nienna, Vána, Nessa, and these were the same in the published version of The Silmarillion with the addition of Este and Vaire. These names remained essentially the same throughout Tolkien’s life, except for a period in the 1930s where Tolkien used Vana (SM/79; LR/206) rather than Vána.

Thus we can see that Tolkien’s conception of his languages contains elements of stability and element of change. The question of “stability” can get quite complex, however. Linguistic elements could be stable in some respects but unstable in others. The forms of words, their semantic meanings, and their etymologies within the internal history of the Elvish language could evolve independently. The name Manwe was fairly stable in both its form and etymological derivation:

  • 1910s: Derived from MANA having to do with morally good things (PE12/58).
  • 1930s: Derived from MAN “holy spirit” (LR/371).
  • 1960s: Derived from MAN “blessed, holy” (PM/357).

Likewise, the name Lórien was always associated with the sense “dream” (PE12/56; LR/370; PE17/48).

In contrast, the names of Varda and Nessa underwent some etymological shifts.

  • Varda: Derived from VṚDṚ having to do with rulership in the 1910s (PE12/102), but derived from BARAD “lofty” in the 1930s and later (LR/351; PE17/22).
  • Nessa: Derived from NETH “young” in the 1930s (LR/376) but from NER “man[ly]” circa 1959-60 (WJ/416).

If we want to evaluate something as nebulous as “conceptual stability”, we need to establish criteria for that evaluation. I believe that evaluating Elvish words themselves would produce an imperfect picture. Individual words can be stable even if their etymologies are not. There are also too many Elvish words to evaluate from a purely practical sense.

I think that examining Elvish roots is a more pragmatic approach to evaluating stability. They are smaller in number, roughly 2000 at the time I did my original analysis. Using roots as a foundation helps expose shifting etymologies. Roots can also show stability in instances where there are gaps in appearances of related Elvish words.

Within his description of Elvish languages, Tolkien described various roots that are the building blocks of primitive Elvish words. These roots represent more ancient forms of the Elvish languages, and consist of a set of determinant consonants and a base vowel. Elvish roots, especially in Tolkien’s later writings, had four basic “shapes” or canta (PE18/33, 84).

  • Uniconsontantal: TA
  • Biconsontal: KAT
  • Triconsontal: KALAT
  • A “4th shape”: KALTA, produced via syncopation of KALATA roots or suffixion of KAT roots.

Tolkien derived most “modern” Elvish words from one or more of these roots, which is generally represented by a “<” or “>” in both the literature and Tolkien’s own writing:

  • Q. carne, S caran “red” < KARAN
  • Q. alcar, S aglar “glory” < KAL “shine”
  • Q. alda, S. galadh “tree” < GALAD

The can be contrasted with the notation “>>” or “<<” which is used to represent revisions or conceptual changes in forms.

Not every root had derivatives in all Elvish languages. The meaning of the “modern” Elvish word may be different from that of its primitive root due to semantic drift, a phenomenon also see in real-world languages. Tolkien’s roots can be compared to the hypothesized roots of Primitive Indo-European, the basis of most modern European and Indian languages (among others). These roots were not necessarily actual word forms in these ancient languages, but are hypothetical forms that were the basis of other actual words.

Tolkien usually gave his roots in ALLCAPS, as shown above. The earliest known source of Elvish roots was the Qenya Lexicon from the 1910s (PE12/29-106), though these were not a perfect representation of primitive Elvish at that conceptual stage, since Tolkien described them in the context of the origins of the Qenya language. The best known collection of roots is The Etymologies composed around 1937 (LR/340-400). There are other documents that collect roots from other conceptual stages. For example Parma Eldalamberon #17 includes a great many roots from various documents in the 1950s and 60s.

Tolkien’s representation of roots in the 1910s and 20s was different from those of the 1930s and later. These early roots were mostly biconsonantal, and triconsonantal roots were rare in the Qenya Lexicon. These early roots often used syllabic consonants, such as KṚN “red” and LḶTḶ “dance” (PE12/48, 55). In Tolkien’s later writings, such roots ofter reappeared as KALAT or KALTA roots, for example KARAN “red” and LILT “dance” from The Etymologies (LR/362, 369).

For purposes of this analysis, I divide Tolkien’s work into three broad conceptual periods:

  • Early (1910s-20s): The period of The Lost Tales, whose major collections of roots were the Qenya and Gnomish Lexicons.
  • Middle (1930s-40s): The period of early Silmarillion and Lord of the Rings drafts, whose major collection of roots was The Etymologies.
  • Late (1950s-70s): The period of the published Lord of the Rings and later Silmarillion drafts, which also includes the conceptual shift from Noldorin to Sindarin.

There is no unified collection of roots from the Late period, though roots regularly appeared in various documents from the 1950s, 60s, and 70s, such as those mentioned above from Parma Eldalamberon #17. The analysis in this article attempts to be comprehensive, covering all published roots from all periods in documents published as of 2021, which is when this research was carried out.

Since we are also analyzing the in-universe evolution of the Elvish languages as Tolkien’s conceived of them, we will use the term “Ancient” or “Primitive” to describe the most ancient forms of the Elvish languages within the Legendarium, and “Modern” to describe the forms of the languages that were contemporaneous with the major narratives. This lets us describe both conceptual (external) and in-universe (internal) chronologies of the Elvish languages, for example:

  • Early Modern forms: The modern forms of the Elvish languages from the 1910s-20s.
  • Late Ancient forms: The ancient forms of the Elvish languages from the 1950s-70s.

For purposes of analysis, my research organized roots by attestations within these three conceptual periods. In most cases, a given root is assigned a representative attestation that best describes the meaning and form of that root within that period. In cases where there are non-trivial conceptual variations inside a particular period, multiple attestations may be combined into the representative attestation.

In the data file for this research, each attestation has a root, gloss, and source. For example: KAL “shine” (Ety/KAL) for the root KAL from The Etymologies of the 1930s. For brevity, redundant glosses are sometimes omitted. Roots are normalized to ALLCAPS, even if they did not appear that way in the original source. Some “roots” may actually be ancient words in cases where no better representative is available. In other cases a modern word form may be the only evidence of a root in a given conceptual period. For example: TINKŌ > Q. tinco “metal” from The Etymologies and The Lord of the Rings.

Roots that are “undefined” or “ephemeral” are excluded from this analysis. Roots with no glosses or derivatives are omitted, even if their form matches earlier or later roots. Deleted (struck through) roots are also omitted. Roots on a rejected page, however, are retained. Deleted roots are also retained if they illustrate continuity with earlier or later forms, marked with {braces}. Only isolated deleted roots are completely omitted.

Within this framework, roots are designated either “stable” or “isolated”. A stable root is one that is attested, either directly or indirectly, across multiple conceptual periods. For example:

  • 1910s: KALA “shine golden” (PE12/44).
  • 1930s: KAL “shine” (Ety/KAL).
  • 1950s-60s: KAL “light, shine” (PE17/24, 50).

An isolated root is one that appears only in a single period, though possibly multiple times within that period:

  • 1910s: LṆQṆ “light on, find, get” (PE12/55).
  • 1930s: SWIN “whirl, eddy” (Ety/SWIN).
  • 1960s: SKEY “pass” (PE22/155).

Stability applies to both form and meaning. A root with the same form is only considered stable if it also retains the same basic meaning. For example, this analysis considers following to be distinct roots:

  • 1910s: NASA “taste nasty” (PE12/64).
  • 1930s: NAS “point, sharp end” (Ety/NAS).
  • 1970: NASAG “*bond, fetter” (PE19/101).

Root glosses do not need to match exactly for a root to be considered stable. Being in the same semantic space is enough, though this does make the stability of meaning somewhat subjective. For some roots, their meaning might be deduced from derived forms. In such cases, the root gloss itself is marked with an asterisk, such as “*bond, fetter” given above, where the meaning of NASAG was deduced from its derivatives Q. naxa “bond, fetter” and S. nadha “fetter”.

This analysis also ignores orthographic variations, such as C vs. K, Q vs. KW, Y vs. (IPA) J. Minor phonetic variations are also ignored. For examples, 1910s roots had voiceless spirants F, Þ, X [H], while starting in the 1930s roots had aspirates PH, TH, KH. These variations can be reasonably ignored since the resulting modern forms end up being the same. For example, the 1910s root FALA can be considered stable when compared to the root PHAL of the 1930s and later, since all these roots produced Gnomish, Noldorin, and Sindarin falas “beach” (PE11/33; Ety/PHAL; PE17/62). The same can be said of Ʒ vs. H, for example ƷO vs. HO, both roots meaning “from” (Ety/ƷŌ̆; WJ/361).

This analysis further distinguishes “partial” and “full” stability. Full stability applies to roots attested in all three conceptual periods, but partial stability applies to roots attested in only two periods:

  • Early Stability: Early + Middle, such as SḶKḶ (PE12/84) and SALAK (Ety/SALÁK) > Q. salqe “grass”.
  • Late Stability: Middle + Late, such as EL “star” (Ety/EL, VT42/11).
  • Skipped Stability: Early + Late, such as ASA “*beside, close to” (PE12/33), ASA “beside” (VT48/25).

This analysis also ranks stability into levels of strength:

  • Strong Stability: With only trivial changes to form and meaning.
    • KALA (PE12/44), KAL (Ety/KAL), KAL (PE17/24) “shine”
  • Moderate Stability: With minor changes to either form or meaning.
    • KṚNṚ (1910s) >> KARAN (1930s+) “red”
    • TULU “fetch, bring; come” (1910s) >> TUL “come, approach” (1930s+)
  • Weak Stability: With major changes to form and/or meaning.
    • SAMA “*room” (1910s) >> STAB “*wood” (1930s)
    • GYEL “*cry of joy” (1930s) >> GYEL “ringing” (1950s)

For analyzing the continuity of roots, one useful concept is that of an “anchor word”. This analysis uses this term for Elvish words whose modern forms are similar even if they are derived from varying roots. For example:

  • Q. carne “red” < KṚNṚ (1910s).
  • Q. carne “red” < KARAN (1930s+).

Such anchor words can assist in tracing otherwise weak stability. For example:

  • Q. sambe “room” < SAMA (1910s).
  • Q. sambe “room” < STAB (1930s).

The anchor words might themselves have contributed to the stability of certain forms. For example, very early Tolkien settled on the form and meaning of the name Glorfindel “Gold-locks”, and this name probably contributed to the stability of Elvish words for “gold” and “hair”. These anchor words are themselves mostly omitted from the article proper, but do appear in the data file of the original research.

As noted previously, sometimes there is only indirect evidence for the existence of a root in a particular conceptual period, implied by attested words from modern forms of Elvish languages. Such modern forms can provide evidence of “implied” stability of roots even when the roots are unattested in particular periods. For example, compare:

  • 1930s: SWES > Q. hwesta “breeze” (Ety/SWES).
  • 1950s: Q. hwesta “breeze” (The Lord of the Rings, Appendix E).

Such implied stability can project both forwards and backwards in time. For example:

  • 1910s: G. lint “quick, agile” (PE11/54).
  • 1940s: (S)LIT “swiftly, speed” (PE22/127).
  • 1950s: Q. linta “swift” (PE17/59).

In the analysis for this article, such modern forms sometimes act as stand-ins for roots whose existence they imply indirectly.

Based on the above criteria, we can assign a score to the stability of a particular root. A fully stable root would have a score of 1, a purely isolated root would have a score of 0, and other variations would have a score somewhere between these two values. We can then use the average score of all roots to give an estimate of conceptual stability. For example, consider a hypothetical Elvish system with exactly 6 roots, two from each conceptual period, 3 fully stable and 3 purely isolated:

EarlyMiddleLate
KALA “shine” (PE12/44)KAL “shine” (Ety/KAL)KAL “shine” (PE17/24, 50)
LṆQṆ “find” (PE12/55)SWIN “whirl” (Ety/SWIN)SKEY “pass” (PE22/155)

Here we have three roots with a score of 1 and three roots with a score of 0, so that the average score is 0.5 representing 50% stability.

To take this approach, we must define a method to assign scores to roots in between fully stable and purely isolated. As a starting point, we will assign a root that appears in all three period a score of 1, and a partially stable root appearing in only two periods a score of 0.7. We will further reduce the score of roots whose stability strength is only moderate (non-trivial changes in either form or meaning) by a factor of ~70%, and roots whose stability strength is only weak (significant changes in form and/or meaning) a factor of ~30%. With a bit of rounding to make the math easier, we can then assign each root a score based on full vs. partial vs. isolated stability and strong vs. moderate vs. weak strength as follows:

  • Full strong 1.0; full moderate 0.7; full weak 0.3
  • Partial strong 0.7; partial moderate 0.5; partial weak 0.2
  • Isolated 0.0

We will assess no penalty for implied stability, where the existence of an ancient root is implied only by attestions of its modern form(s). For roots appearing in all three periods, a mixture of stronger and weaker stability penalizes only the variants. For example:

EarlyMiddleLate
KṚNṚ “*red” (PE12/48)KARAN “red” (Ety/KARÁN)S. caran “red” (PE17/36)
Score = 0.7Score = 1Score = 1

This gives us an aggregate average score of 48.4%.

Of course, this calculation depends on agreement on the basic scoring methodology. Different scoring methods would get another result. This score also hand-waves past other variations. Changes in words, grammar, phonology, etc. are not addressed. A more fair representation might be 40-55% stability to accommodate the subjectivity of the scores. Nevertheless, this score may still be higher or lower than you expect.
